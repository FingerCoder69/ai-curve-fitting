Parametric Curve Optimization

This repository contains my solution and methodology for the AI/R&D curve-fitting assignment.

The goal of this project was to extract three hidden variables ($\theta$, $M$, and $X$) from a dataset of spatial coordinates (xy_data.csv) generated by a complex parametric equation.

2. Methodology & Thought Process

The Roadblock: The Missing Variable

My initial plan was to treat this as a standard curve-fitting problem: define the parametric equations in Python, calculate the L1 distance against the target CSV data, and use SciPy's differential_evolution to minimize the loss.

However, when I inspected xy_data.csv, I realized the $t$ (time) parameter was completely omitted. Without knowing when a specific $(x,y)$ point was generated, I couldn't align my predicted points with the target points. A standard optimizer cannot evaluate parametric equations without the parameter, meaning I couldn't calculate the L1 loss directly.

I knew treating $t$ as a massive set of latent variables would create an incredibly slow, computationally expensive search space prone to local minima. I needed to extract $t$ dynamically from the spatial coordinates themselves.

The Pivot: Rigid Body Transformation

I took a closer look at the provided equations. The term $e^{M\vert{}t\vert{}} \cdot \sin(0.3t)$ is highly non-linear and complex. To clarify the structure, I substituted it with a placeholder, $U$.

$x = t \cdot \cos(\theta) - U \cdot \sin(\theta) + X$

$y = t \cdot \sin(\theta) + U \cdot \cos(\theta) + 42$

By isolating $x$ and $y$, I had an "aha!" moment: the equations represent a rigid body transformation. The original curve coordinates $(t, U)$ are simply being multiplied by a standard 2D Rotation Matrix for angle $\theta$, and then translated by the vector $(X, 42)$.

The Math: Inverse Rotation

Because the forward equations apply a rigid body transformation, I realized I could mathematically reverse it. By subtracting the translation and applying an inverse rotation, I could completely cancel out the complex $U$ term.

Step 1: Subtract the translation constants, then multiply the $x$-equation by $\cos(\theta)$ and the $y$-equation by $\sin(\theta)$:

$(x - X)\cos(\theta) = t \cdot \cos^2(\theta) - U \cdot \sin(\theta)\cos(\theta)$

$(y - 42)\sin(\theta) = t \cdot \sin^2(\theta) + U \cdot \cos(\theta)\sin(\theta)$

Step 2: Add the two equations together. The complex $U$ terms are exact opposites, so they cancel out to zero:

$(x - X)\cos(\theta) + (y - 42)\sin(\theta) = t(\cos^2(\theta) + \sin^2(\theta))$

Step 3: Apply the Pythagorean identity ($\cos^2(\theta) + \sin^2(\theta) = 1$) to yield the clean, isolated formula for time:

$t = (x - X)\cos(\theta) + (y - 42)\sin(\theta)$

The Code: Custom Loss Function

With $t$ isolated algebraically, the optimization became highly efficient.

I built a custom loss function where the optimizer passes in its current guesses for $\theta$ and $X$. I use those guesses to dynamically calculate the $t$ array for every point in the CSV. Then, I plug that dynamic $t$ array back into the original parametric equations to generate my predicted coordinates and calculate the L1 distance.

By applying SciPy's differential_evolution to this time-independent loss function, the algorithm smoothly bypassed the missing data problem and rapidly converged on the exact hidden variables.
